## Summary

OpenAI president Greg Brockman disclosed in court testimony on May 5 that the company expects to spend $50 billion on computing power in 2026 — up from ~$30 million in 2017. The testimony was made during OpenAI's lawsuit against Elon Musk, and The Register notes the money is investor capital rather than OpenAI revenue, with the company still unprofitable. Recent mega-investments from Amazon ($50B), NVIDIA ($30B), and SoftBank ($30B) are reportedly contingent on OpenAI purchasing compute capacity through each investor's infrastructure.

## Key Highlights

- $50B compute spend projected for 2026, up from ~$30M in 2017 — a roughly 1,600× increase over nine years
- Disclosed in court testimony during the OpenAI vs. Elon Musk lawsuit, not a planned announcement
- OpenAI remains unprofitable and has struggled to meet its own revenue targets
- Key investment strings attached: Amazon's $50B pledge contingent on renting 2GW of AWS accelerators; NVIDIA's $30B tied to deploying 5GW of compute (~$300B estimated cost)
- Combined $110B from Amazon, NVIDIA, and SoftBank came with $80B in compute-deployment conditions

## Why It Matters

OpenAI's compute economics reveal a circular dependency — major investors are essentially subsidizing capacity purchases from their own infrastructure, raising questions about whether the $50B represents genuine growth or structured self-dealing.
